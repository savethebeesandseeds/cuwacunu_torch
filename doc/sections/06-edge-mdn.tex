\section{Edge Forecast Head}
\label{sec:edge-forecast-head}

The edge forecast head is a mixture density network in the sense of
Bishop~\cite{bishop1994mdn}.  Instead of predicting only one future value, it
predicts a conditional probability law for each future target vector.  This is
important because the future market state may be multi-modal: several different
future moves can be plausible under the same current representation.

The head consumes the representation exported by
Section~\ref{sec:representation}.  The head itself expects one $D_e$-dimensional slice per
sample,
\[
  \tensor{z}^{b} \in \R^{D_e},
  \qquad
  \tensor{z}\in\R^{B\times D_e}.
\]
If the encoder emits a sequence representation
$\tensor{s}^{b}\in\R^{H_x'\times D_e}$, then a temporal reducer is applied before
the MDN head:
\[
  \tensor{z}^{b} = r_{\theta}(\tensor{s}^{b},\tensor{m}_x^{b}) \in \R^{D_e}.
\]
Thus the MDN does not operate directly on the rank-four input tensor.  It
operates on the learned edge representation.

\subsection{Backbone and per-channel heads}
\label{subsec:mdn-backbone-and-per-channel-heads}

Let $H\in\N$ be the hidden width of the MDN backbone and let
$R_{\mathrm{res}}\in\N\cup\{0\}$ be the number of residual MLP blocks.  The
first layer maps the representation to the hidden space:
\[
  \vect{h}^{(0)}_b
  =
  \operatorname{SiLU}
  \left(
    \mat{W}_{0}\tensor{z}^{b}+\vect{a}_{0}
  \right)
  \in \R^{H}.
\]
Each residual block keeps the same width $H$.  A typical block has the form
\[
  \vect{u}^{(r)}_b
  =
  \operatorname{SiLU}
  \left(
    \mat{A}_{r}
    \operatorname{LN}_{r\,1}(\vect{h}^{(r-1)}_b)
    +
    \vect{a}_{r}
  \right)
  \in \R^{2H},
\]
\[
  \widetilde{\vect{h}}^{(r)}_b
  =
  \mat{B}_{r}\vect{u}^{(r)}_b+\vect{b}_{r}
  \in \R^{H},
\]
and
\[
  \vect{h}^{(r)}_b
  =
  \operatorname{SiLU}
  \left(
    \operatorname{LN}_{r\,2}
    \left(
      \vect{h}^{(r-1)}_b
      +
      \widetilde{\vect{h}}^{(r)}_b
    \right)
  \right)
  \in \R^{H}.
\]
After the residual stack, the backbone output is
\[
  \vect{u}_b
  =
  \operatorname{LN}_{\mathrm{out}}
  \left(
    \vect{h}^{(R_{\mathrm{res}})}_b
  \right)
  \in \R^{H}.
\]

The current head uses one output head per channel.  Conditional on the shared
backbone vector $\vect{u}_b$, channel $c$ has three linear maps:
\[
  \mat{W}^{(\alpha)}_c:\R^{H}\rightarrow\R^{H_fK},
  \qquad
  \mat{W}^{(\mu)}_c:\R^{H}\rightarrow\R^{H_fKD_f},
  \qquad
  \mat{W}^{(\beta)}_c:\R^{H}\rightarrow\R^{H_fKD_f}.
\]
After reshaping, these maps produce
\[
  \alpha^{b\,c\,i\,k}\in\R,
  \qquad
  \mat{\mu}^{b\,c\,i\,k}\in\R^{D_f},
  \qquad
  \vect{\beta}^{b\,c\,i\,k}\in\R^{D_f},
\]
for every channel $c=1,\ldots,C$, forecast step $i=1,\ldots,H_f$, and mixture
component $k=1,\ldots,K$.

Equivalently,
\[
  g_{\psi} : \R^{D_e}
  \rightarrow
  \R^{C \times H_f \times K}
  \times
  \R^{C \times H_f \times K \times D_f}
  \times
  \R^{C \times H_f \times K \times D_f},
\]
with
\[
  g_{\psi}(\tensor{z}^{b})
  =
  \left\{
    \alpha^{b\,c\,i\,k},
    \mat{\mu}^{b\,c\,i\,k},
    \vect{\beta}^{b\,c\,i\,k}
  \right\}_{c\,i\,k}.
\]

In batch form, the tensors have shapes
\[
  \mat{\alpha}\in\R^{B\times C\times H_f\times K},
\]
\[
  \mat{\mu}\in\R^{B\times C\times H_f\times K\times D_f},
  \qquad
  \mat{\sigma}\in\Rpos^{B\times C\times H_f\times K\times D_f}.
\]
The implementation may store normalized log-weights
$\log\pi^{b\,c\,i\,k}$ instead of raw logits $\alpha^{b\,c\,i\,k}$ for numerical
stability.  Mathematically these are related by
\[
  \pi^{b\,c\,i\,k}
  =
  \frac{\exp(\alpha^{b\,c\,i\,k})}
       {\sum_{\ell=1}^{K}\exp(\alpha^{b\,c\,i\,\ell})}.
\]

\subsection{Mixture parameterization}
\label{subsec:mdn-mixture-parameterization}

For each sample, channel, and forecast horizon, the MDN defines a $K$-component
Gaussian mixture over the selected future target vector
$\tensor{f}^{b\,c\,i}\in\R^{D_f}$.  The mixture weights are
\[
  \mat{\pi}^{b\,c\,i}
  =
  \softmax(\mat{\alpha}^{b\,c\,i})
  \in [0,1]^K,
  \qquad
  \sum_{k=1}^{K}\pi^{b\,c\,i\,k}=1.
\]
The component scales are made positive coordinatewise:
\[
  \mat{\sigma}^{b\,c\,i\,k}
  =
  \softplus(\vect{\beta}^{b\,c\,i\,k})+\eps
  \in\Rpos^{D_f},
  \qquad
  \eps\in\Rpos.
\]
The current component covariance family is diagonal:
\[
  \mat{\Sigma}^{b\,c\,i\,k}
  =
  \diag
  \left(
    (\mat{\sigma}^{b\,c\,i\,k})^{2}
  \right)
  \in\R^{D_f\times D_f}.
\]
Therefore the predictive law for one future vector is
\[
  p_{\psi}(\tensor{f}^{b\,c\,i}\given\tensor{z}^{b})
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^{K}
  \pi^{b\,c\,i\,k}
  \mathcal{N}
  \left(
    \tensor{f}^{b\,c\,i};
    \mat{\mu}^{b\,c\,i\,k},
    \mat{\Sigma}^{b\,c\,i\,k}
  \right).
\]
For the edge carried by sample $b$, write
\[
  \mathcal{D}_{b\,c\,i}
  :=
  p_{\psi}(\cdot\given\tensor{z}^{b})
  \in\mathcal{P}(\R^{D_f}).
\]
If sample $b$ corresponds to edge $e\in\mathcal{E}$, then the same predictive
law may be written in edge-indexed form as
\[
  \mathcal{D}_{b\,c\,i}
  =
  \mathcal{D}_{b\,c\,i}.
\]

The diagonal covariance assumption is inside each Gaussian component.  The
mixture distribution itself can still have non-diagonal covariance because
different mixture components may have different mean vectors.

\subsection{Future target selection}
\label{subsec:mdn-future-target-selection}

The future tensor may contain more coordinates than the MDN is asked to model.
Let
\[
  \mathcal{J}
  =
  (j_1,\ldots,j_{D_f})
\]
be the configured list of target feature indices.  If the raw future tensor has
width $D_x$, then the supervised target used by the MDN is
\[
  f^{b\,c\,i\,d}
  =
  f_{\mathrm{raw}}^{b\,c\,i\,j_d},
  \qquad
  d=1,\ldots,D_f.
\]
After this selection, the target tensor has shape
\[
  \tensor{f}\in\R^{B\times C\times H_f\times D_f},
  \qquad
  \tensor{m}_f\in\{0,1\}^{B\times C\times H_f}.
\]
The future mask indicates which target positions are valid and should
contribute to the likelihood.

\subsection{Training loss}
\label{subsec:mdn-training-loss}

For one valid target position $(b,c,i)$, the unweighted MDN negative
log-likelihood is
\[
  \ell^{b\,c\,i}
  =
  -
  \log
  \left[
    \sum_{k=1}^{K}
    \pi^{b\,c\,i\,k}
    \prod_{d=1}^{D_f}
    \mathcal{N}
    \left(
      f^{b\,c\,i\,d};
      \mu^{b\,c\,i\,k\,d},
      (\sigma^{b\,c\,i\,k\,d})^{2}
    \right)
  \right].
\]
Equivalently, in log-sum-exp form,
\[
  \ell^{b\,c\,i}
  =
  -
  \operatorname{logsumexp}_{k}
  \left(
    \log \pi^{b\,c\,i\,k}
    +
    \sum_{d=1}^{D_f}
    \log
    \mathcal{N}
    \left(
      f^{b\,c\,i\,d};
      \mu^{b\,c\,i\,k\,d},
      (\sigma^{b\,c\,i\,k\,d})^{2}
    \right)
  \right).
\]
This is the standard maximum-likelihood training objective for the conditional
mixture model.

The implementation may also use feature weights
$w^{(f)}_d\in\Rnonneg$.  In that case the per-component log-density is replaced
by the weighted pseudo-likelihood score
\[
  q^{b\,c\,i\,k}
  =
  \sum_{d=1}^{D_f}
  w^{(f)}_d
  \log
  \mathcal{N}
  \left(
    f^{b\,c\,i\,d};
    \mu^{b\,c\,i\,k\,d},
    (\sigma^{b\,c\,i\,k\,d})^{2}
  \right),
\]
and
\[
  \ell_{(w)}^{b\,c\,i}
  =
  -
  \operatorname{logsumexp}_{k}
  \left(
    \log \pi^{b\,c\,i\,k}
    +
    q^{b\,c\,i\,k}
  \right).
\]
When all feature weights are equal to one,
$\ell_{(w)}^{b\,c\,i}$ reduces to the ordinary MDN negative log-likelihood.

Let $w^{(c)}_c\in\Rnonneg$ be an optional channel weight and let
$w^{(h)}_i\in\Rnonneg$ be an optional horizon weight.  The masked batch loss is
\[
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{MDN}}
  =
  \frac{
    \sum_{b=1}^{B}
    \sum_{c=1}^{C}
    \sum_{i=1}^{H_f}
    m_f^{b\,c\,i}
    w^{(c)}_c
    w^{(h)}_i
    \ell_{(w)}^{b\,c\,i}
  }{
    \sum_{b=1}^{B}
    \sum_{c=1}^{C}
    \sum_{i=1}^{H_f}
    m_f^{b\,c\,i}
    w^{(c)}_c
    w^{(h)}_i
  }.
\]
Only valid future positions contribute to the numerator.  Implementations may
clamp the denominator away from zero for numerical safety.

The scale used inside the loss may also be protected by floors and ceilings,
for example, with $\sigma_{\min}\in\Rpos$,
\[
  \bar{\sigma}^{b\,c\,i\,k\,d}
  =
  \max
  \left(
    \sigma_{\min},
    \sigma^{b\,c\,i\,k\,d}
  \right),
\]
with an optional upper clamp
$\bar{\sigma}^{b\,c\,i\,k\,d}\le\sigma_{\max}$ for
$\sigma_{\max}\in\Rpos$.  These protections do not change the mathematical
object being modeled; they make numerical training stable.

\subsection{Expectation and covariance observers}
\label{subsec:mdn-expectation-and-covariance}

The expected future vector is available in closed form:
\[
  \widehat{\tensor{f}}^{b\,c\,i}
  =
  \E_{\psi}
  \left[
    \tensor{f}^{b\,c\,i}\given\tensor{z}^{b}
  \right]
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^{K}
  \pi^{b\,c\,i\,k}
  \mat{\mu}^{b\,c\,i\,k}
  \in\R^{D_f}.
\]
In batch form,
\[
  \widehat{\tensor{f}}
  \in
  \R^{B\times C\times H_f\times D_f}.
\]

The full covariance of the mixture law is also closed form:
\[
  \operatorname{Cov}_{\psi}
  \left[
    \tensor{f}^{b\,c\,i}
    \given
    \tensor{z}^{b}
  \right]
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^{K}
  \pi^{b\,c\,i\,k}
  \left(
    \mat{\Sigma}^{b\,c\,i\,k}
    +
    \mat{\mu}^{b\,c\,i\,k}(\mat{\mu}^{b\,c\,i\,k})^{\top}
  \right)
  -
  \widehat{\tensor{f}}^{b\,c\,i}
  (\widehat{\tensor{f}}^{b\,c\,i})^{\top}.
\]
Although each component covariance $\mat{\Sigma}^{b\,c\,i\,k}$ is diagonal, this
mixture covariance can contain off-diagonal terms through the component means.

\subsection{Scalar edge-return marginal}
\label{subsec:mdn-scalar-edge-return-marginal}

Some downstream graph operations use one scalar return coordinate rather than
the full future vector.  Let $d_r\in\{1,\ldots,D_f\}$ be the selected return
coordinate and define
\[
  r^{b\,c\,i}
  =
  f^{b\,c\,i\,d_r}
  \in\R.
\]
The scalar predictive law is the marginal mixture
\[
  \mathcal{D}^{(r)}_{b\,c\,i}
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^{K}
  \pi^{b\,c\,i\,k}
  \mathcal{N}
  \left(
    r;
    \mu^{b\,c\,i\,k\,d_r},
    (\sigma^{b\,c\,i\,k\,d_r})^{2}
  \right)
  \in\mathcal{P}(\R).
\]
Its expectation is
\[
  \widehat{r}^{b\,c\,i}
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^{K}
  \pi^{b\,c\,i\,k}
  \mu^{b\,c\,i\,k\,d_r},
\]
and its variance is
\[
  \Var_{\psi}
  \left[
    r^{b\,c\,i}
    \given
    \tensor{z}^{b}
  \right]
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^{K}
  \pi^{b\,c\,i\,k}
  \left(
    (\sigma^{b\,c\,i\,k\,d_r})^{2}
    +
    (\mu^{b\,c\,i\,k\,d_r})^{2}
  \right)
  -
  (\widehat{r}^{b\,c\,i})^{2}.
\]
These scalar laws are the objects read by the edge observers in
Section~\ref{sec:distribution-field-and-observer-operators} and by the
edge-to-node recovery step in Section~\ref{sec:edges-to-nodes}.

\subsection{Scope of the head}
\label{subsec:mdn-head-scope}

The MDN head is edge-local.  It defines
\[
  \mathcal{D}_{b\,c\,i}
  =
  p_{\psi}(\cdot\given\tensor{z}^{b})
\]
for each sample, channel, and forecast step.  It does not by itself define a
joint probability law over all active edges, all channels, or all horizons.
When the training loss sums the NLL over $(c,i)$, it is using a pointwise
conditional likelihood over channel-horizon targets.  A joint cross-edge or
cross-horizon uncertainty model is a separate design choice, not part of the
current standardized MDN head.
